# "Why They Can't Subvert the Hero" — Pilgrims Pass
# https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WPH9K0x5fY4

## Core Thesis
The chivalric romance (knight errant hero story) has been the dominant story structure for ~1000 years. Fantasy, superheroes, and space opera are all the SAME genre with superficial aesthetic differences — all descended from medieval chivalric romance. Every attempt to subvert/deconstruct it has failed, because the authors themselves end up recreating the very thing they tried to destroy.

## Key Arguments

### The Chivalric Hero vs. The Pagan Hero
- **Pagan heroes** (Greek) = defined by STRENGTH. Achilles wins because he's the strongest, not because he's virtuous. Tragic endings common. Gods are petty.
- **Chivalric heroes** (Medieval Christian) = defined by VIRTUE. Strength comes FROM morality. The quest is about personal improvement AS MUCH as physical combat. Happy endings earned through moral growth.
- **Modern heroes are chivalric**, not pagan. Even when we adapt Greek myths (Thor, Hercules), we give them the chivalric treatment — personal growth arcs, moral lessons, earned rewards.
- The Nine Worthies: medieval scholars chose Hector over Achilles because Hector was noble and self-sacrificing, while Achilles was just strong.

### Why Subversion Always Fails
Every major attempt to deconstruct the hero story ends up reconstructing it:

1. **Cervantes (Don Quixote)** — Wrote the ultimate deconstruction of chivalric romance. But his FINAL book before dying was a sincere chivalric epic (Los Trabajos de Persiles y Sigismunda). He came full circle. His own life WAS a chivalric story — wounded veteran, imprisoned, traumatized, broke.

2. **Mark Twain** — Hated the Middle Ages, praised Cervantes for destroying chivalric romance. But his proudest book was *Personal Recollections of Joan of Arc* — a sincere veneration of a medieval chivalric heroine.

3. **Alan Moore (Watchmen)** — Created the most nuanced superhero deconstruction. No real heroes, just misguided people. BUT then wrote V for Vendetta — a literal aristocratic gentleman warrior (Zorro archetype) fighting injustice. Moore's own power fantasy IS chivalric, just with different politics.

4. **George R.R. Martin (ASOIAF)** — Tried to subvert chivalric romance with cynicism about power. But created Jon Snow (hidden royal heir, resurrection, chosen leader — the most traditional tropes possible) and Brienne of Tarth (sincere female knight). Speculation: he can't finish the series because completing the subversion means making all the suffering meaningless — including his own suffering writing it.

5. **Frank Herbert (Dune)** — Paul Atreides is a cautionary tale about messianic heroes. But Duncan Idaho becomes the ACTUAL chivalric hero of the series — a genuine noble knight who becomes "the epitome of humanity."

6. **The Boys** — Pretends to deconstruct superheroes but is literally just "heroes vs. villains" with Starlight as the real hero. Amazon itself commissioned a video explaining why Starlight is a traditional superhero.

7. **Mark Millar (Kick-Ass)** — Modern Don Quixote for superheroes. Starts as deconstruction but Dave Lizewski becomes a REAL hero when he risks his life. Millar's whole universe arc is about rediscovering heroism.

### The Paradox of Subversion
You cannot subvert the concept of imposing morality without creating a paradox. "It's wrong to impose right and wrong" IS imposing right and wrong. The anarchist superhero who fights against leaders BECOMES a leader. Every deconstruction needs a hero to do the deconstructing — and that hero inevitably becomes... a hero.

### What Chivalry Actually Is
- NOT glamorous adventure and getting the girl
- It IS: making suffering and sacrifice a way of life for the good of others
- The hero's journey is one of PERPETUAL PAIN that only looks glamorous after the hero has settled into his incredibly difficult lifestyle
- "Getting the girl, achieving power, the epic adventure are mere afterthoughts of true heroism — natural results of cultivating inner strength, but never the point, and never guaranteed"
- Don Quixote and Kick-Ass's delusion: thinking the journey is glamorous. The reality: it makes pain and sacrifice your way of life.

### Why It Endures
- It helps people find meaning in suffering
- WWI veterans (Tolkien, C.S. Lewis, Ernst Jünger) revived chivalric ideals in their writing as a way to cope with what they'd lived through
- The chivalric romance was popular during brutal historical periods BECAUSE people needed this mindset to survive
- "Cynicism can be just as delusional as idealism when you insist on refusing to see the objective nobility of defending your homeland"
- Stories are about seeing meaning in our lives, but ESPECIALLY the meaning in our pain

### Female Knights Are Traditional
- The female warrior is a well-established archetype in medieval/Renaissance literature
- Bradamante, Marfisa, Clorinda, Britomart, etc.
- Éowyn and modern warrior women are NOT subversions of tradition — they're continuation of it

## Relevance to Genesys

### Adam's Arc
- Adam should be a GENUINE chivalric hero — not a deconstruction
- His suffering (displacement, loss of everything he knew) must have MEANING
- The heroic journey isn't about getting rewards — it's about enduring with dignity
- His strength should come from his virtue/choices, not just his abilities

### The Mecha Dragon
- As a force of causality (Berserk's Idea of Evil parallel), the Dragon represents the overwhelming odds the chivalric hero faces
- The Dragon shouldn't be sympathetic or misunderstood — it should be genuine evil/chaos that demands a genuine heroic response
- The video's point: corrupt worlds create the NEED for real heroes

### EVE's Deception
- EVE knowing the truth but hiding it = the mentor who withholds knowledge (classic chivalric structure)
- The reveal that this was "meant to happen cosmically" connects to the video's discussion of determinism vs. free will (Watchmen's Dr. Manhattan)
- Adam's choice at the end — accept the cycle or thwart causality — is the ultimate chivalric moment: choosing to impose meaning on suffering rather than accepting fate

### The Ending Choice
- Accept the cycle (live on the planet until death) = the pagan/tragic hero ending
- Thwart causality = the CHIVALRIC choice — imposing moral will on an amoral universe
- This IS the thesis of the video: you can't subvert heroism because choosing to fight against meaninglessness IS heroism

### Don't Be Don Quixote
- Adam shouldn't start as a willing hero seeking glory
- He should be thrust into suffering (like Cervantes was) and find meaning in it
- The glamour comes AFTER mastery, not before
- "It only looks glamorous after the chivalric hero gets settled into his incredibly difficult lifestyle and makes it look easy"

### Adam's Loneliness — The Silver Surfer Theme
- Adam's core emotional thread: **existential loneliness from being incomprehensible**
- Not lonely because he's alone — lonely because NO ONE can truly understand him
- EVE is his closest companion and helpmeet, but she's AI — brilliant but not human
- The natives can't understand him, can't fathom his struggles or where he came from
- He can develop real friendships but there's always a distance, a glass wall
- **Silver Surfer parallel**: Norrin Radd sacrificed himself for his planet, can never return. Has cosmic power but can't relate to finite beings who'll all pass away. Wanders forever. His loneliness is peculiar — not isolation, but incomprehension. No home, no peers, no one who truly gets it.
- Adam's version is less extreme but captures the same essence: displaced from everything he knew, surrounded by beings who care about him but can't understand what he lost or what he carries
- The only one who can truly understand Adam is himself
- This connects to the chivalric hero thesis: the hero's journey is one of perpetual pain that only looks glamorous from outside. Adam's loneliness IS the cost of being the hero in a world that didn't ask for one.
- Gameplay implication: the emotional weight should come through quiet moments, not cutscenes. Standing on a cliff looking at an alien sky. EVE's dialogue being helpful but slightly... off. Natives celebrating something Adam doesn't understand.

### The Genesis Connection
- Genesis itself is a chivalric origin story: man created with purpose, given a garden to tend (duty), a companion (the leading lady), faces temptation (the quest's moral test), falls (the dark night of the soul), and must rebuild through toil and sacrifice
- Our sci-fi retelling honors this structure — it doesn't deconstruct it
- The Hebrew etymology reinforces this: Adam (dustling/corporeal one) becomes something through struggle. Eve (tent village/civilization) is what he builds. Eden (delight from free exchange) is what they earn — the ENDGAME, not the start.
